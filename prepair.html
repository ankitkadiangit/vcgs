Prepair & prepair+

Thinking about having a baby or currently pregnant?

Understanding what's in your genes can help you make informed choices about family planning.

Genetic carrier screening tells you about your chance of having a child with an inherited genetic condition. If you have a high chance, you can consider options like IVF and donor egg/sperm. Carrier screening is relevant to everyone planning a pregnancy.

If you have a high chance, you can consider options like IVF and donor egg/sperm.

Carrier screening is relevant to everyone planning a pregnancy.

5 things to know about carrier screening

  • Carrier screening involves testing you and your reproductive partner to find out if you are carriers for a genetic condition.
  • Having one faulty copy and one working copy of a gene is called being a carrier. Being a carrier usually does not affect your health.
  • Most people who are identified as a carrier have no family history of the condition.
  • If you and your reproductive partner are both carriers for the same condition, you usually have a 25% or 1 in 4 chance of having a child with that condition.
  • Knowing this information can be useful when starting a family.

How to arrange testing through VCGS

Step 1

Talk to your doctor about genetic carrier screening.

If this test is appropriate, they will complete a test request form.

Step 2

Testing can be performed using blood or saliva.

Visit our website to find blood collection sites OR to arrange a saliva kit to be sent to you.

Step 2

Email a copy of your request form to carrierscreening@vcgs.org.au and we will send you the specific saliva kits.

If you’re already pregnant, we may organise blood samples from both partners instead.

Step 3

Results will be available from your doctor.

In some cases, your doctor might refer you to one of our genetic counsellors for your results.
